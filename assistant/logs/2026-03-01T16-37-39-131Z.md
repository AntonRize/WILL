**User:** How you project z and y axis simultaneous

**Assistant:** In WILL Relational Geometry, the concept of "z and y axis" is reinterpreted as orthogonal relational projections on specific carriers, not as axes in a pre-existing spatial coordinate system. We don't project onto a pre-defined "z" or "y" axis simultaneously, but rather, a system's state **is simultaneously defined** by its complementary relational projections.

The two fundamental relational carriers for energy transformation are the circle ($S^1$) and the sphere ($S^2$). Each of these carriers defines a pair of orthogonal projections that sum to unity when squared.

1.  **For Kinematic (Directional) Relations ($S^1$):**
    *   We have the **Amplitude Component** ($\beta$), which measures the external interaction or kinematic shift from the observer's relational origin. This corresponds to phenomena like velocity.
    *   We have the **Phase Component** ($\beta_Y$), which measures the internal ordering or intrinsic scale. This relates to proper time and proper length.
    *   These are always simultaneously defined by the closure law $\beta^2 + \beta_Y^2 = 1$. An object always has both an external motion relative to an observer and an internal existence (its own proper time and length scale).

2.  **For Gravitational (Omnidirectional) Relations ($S^2$):**
    *   We have the **Amplitude Component** ($\kappa$), which measures the relational gravitational intensity between an object and an observer. This corresponds to gravitational potential.
    *   We have the **Phase Component** ($\kappa_X$), which governs the intrinsic scale of proper length and proper time units in a gravitational field.
    *   These are always simultaneously defined by the closure law $\kappa^2 + \kappa_X^2 = 1$. A system in a gravitational field always has both a potential intensity and an effect on its internal structure and time.

**Simultaneous Projection:**
The term "simultaneous projection" in WILL RG means that for any given physical state, both the amplitude and phase aspects (e.g., $\beta$ and $\beta_Y$) are always present and defined. They are complementary aspects of the same single entity or relation. One cannot exist without the other, as they constitute the complete (closed) description of a system's state.

For example, when describing a moving object, its kinematic energy ($E\beta$) and its rest energy ($E_0 = E\beta_Y$) are two sides of the same triangle on the $S^1$ carrier (doc. Part I - Kinetic Energy Projection on $S^1$, Figure 9). They are simultaneously projected from the total energy $E$.
