\subsection{The 130-Year Limitation}
For more than a century, radial-velocity and astrometric orbital analyses have been limited by the \textbf{$M \sin i$} (or equivalently $\beta \sin i$) degeneracy. The observed velocity semi-amplitude $K$ only constrains the product
\[
K = \frac{\beta_{\rm int} \sin i}{\sqrt{1-e^2}},
\]
leaving an infinite family of physically distinct $(\beta, i)$ pairs consistent with the data. This degeneracy has been the principal obstacle to model-independent mass and inclination determination in exoplanet, binary, and Galactic-center studies.


By deriving Relational Orbital Mechanics (R.O.M. \ref{sec:rom}) RG sidesteps this degeneracy at the algebraic level by introducing two independent higher-order kinematic invariants that depend on $\beta$ (or $\beta_{\rm int}$) alone, without reference to the observer's inclination $i$.

\subsubsection{The Observer Equations at Extrema}

The raw spectroscopic shift observed by a telescope, $Z_{raw}(o)$, is the product of the line-of-sight Doppler projection and the systemic transverse baseline $Z_{sys}(o)$. The extrema of the radial velocity occur at phases $o_{i1} = -\omega_i$ (maximum redshift) and $o_{i2} = \pi - \omega_i$ (minimum redshift/maximum blue shift).

Utilizing the relational energy invariant $\kappa_o(o)^2 - \beta_o(o)^2 = \beta^2$, we eliminate the gravitational projection $\kappa_o$ entirely. Because $Z_{sys}(o) = 1/\tau(o)$, the systemic spacetime phase factors at the extrema, $\tau(-\omega_i)$ and $\tau(\pi-\omega_i)$, become strict functions of the global kinematic projection $\beta$, eccentricity $e$, and the argument of periapsis $\omega_i$:

\begin{align}
\tau(-\omega_i) &= \sqrt{1 - 2\beta^2\frac{1+e\cos\omega_i}{1-e^2}} \sqrt{1 - \beta^2\frac{1+e^2+2e\cos\omega_i}{1-e^2}} \\
\tau(\pi-\omega_i) &= \sqrt{1 - 2\beta^2\frac{1-e\cos\omega_i}{1-e^2}} \sqrt{1 - \beta^2\frac{1+e^2-2e\cos\omega_i}{1-e^2}}
\end{align}

The observed raw extrema can then be written linearly with respect to the classical semi-amplitude invariant $K_i = \frac{\beta\sin i}{\sqrt{1-e^2}}$:
\begin{align}
Z_{raw}(-\omega_i) \cdot \tau(-\omega_i) &= 1 + K_i(1+e\cos\omega_i) \label{eq:zmax} \\
Z_{raw}(\pi-\omega_i) \cdot \tau(\pi-\omega_i) &= 1 - K_i(1-e\cos\omega_i) \label{eq:zmin}
\end{align}

\subsubsection{Algebraic Decoupling of $\beta$ and $i$}

To break the degeneracy, we isolate the inclination-dependent term. Subtracting Equation \ref{eq:zmin} from Equation \ref{eq:zmax} yields  twice the semi-amplitude:
\begin{equation}
2K_i = Z_{raw}(-\omega_i) \tau(-\omega_i) - Z_{raw}(\pi-\omega_i) \tau(\pi-\omega_i) \label{eq:2Ki}
\end{equation}

Adding the two equations yields:
\begin{equation}
Z_{raw}(-\omega_i) \tau(-\omega_i) + Z_{raw}(\pi-\omega_i) \tau(\pi-\omega_i) = 2 + 2K_i e\cos\omega_i
\end{equation}

Substituting $2K_i$ from Equation \ref{eq:2Ki} into this sum and grouping the $Z_{raw}$ terms isolates the fundamental \textbf{Decryption Invariant}:

\hypertarget{eq:decryption_invariant}{}\label{eq:decryption_invariant}
\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=white, colframe=black, title=The Decryption Invariant]
\begin{equation}
Z_{raw}(-\omega_i) \tau(-\omega_i) (1 - e\cos\omega_i) + Z_{raw}(\pi-\omega_i) \tau(\pi-\omega_i) (1 + e\cos\omega_i) = 2
\end{equation}
\end{tcolorbox}


This equation contains the raw observational signals, the known geometry ($e$), and the internal R.O.M. variables ($\beta, \omega_i$) locked inside the $\tau$ functions. Crucially, \textbf{the inclination angle $i$ has been strictly eliminated}. This proves analytically that the true kinematic projection $\beta$ is determined entirely by the asymmetry of the transverse baselines at the extrema, independent of the observer's viewing angle.

\subsubsection{Analytical Extraction of $\sin i$}

Once $\beta$ and $\omega_i$ are constrained via Equation \ref{eq:decryption_invariant} (and verified via the balance phase $O_o$), the true orbital inclination is trivially extracted by substituting the definition of $K_i$ back into Equation \ref{eq:2Ki}:

\subsection{Algebraic Isolation of the Balance Nodes}
\hypertarget{sec:balance_nodes}{}\label{sec:balance_nodes}

\begin{definition}[Balance Phases]

\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The condition $\beta_{los} = 0$ requires the geometric term to vanish:
\[ \cos(o_b + \omega_i) + e\cos\omega_i = 0 \]
Let $u = o_b + \omega_i$. Then $\cos u = -e\cos\omega_i$ and $\sin u = \pm \sqrt{1 - e^2\cos^2\omega_i}$.
We define the geometric node parameter $\Theta_i$ as the strict limit of this orthogonality:
\begin{equation}
\Theta_i \equiv \sqrt{1 - e^2\cos^2\omega_i}
\end{equation}
Applying the cosine difference identity $\cos o_b = \cos(u - \omega_i)$:
\[ \cos o_{b1,2} = -e\cos^2\omega_i \pm \Theta_i\sin\omega_i \]

We substitute this strictly into the phase factor $(1 + e\cos o_b)$ for the $S^2$ potential projection:
\[ 1 + e\cos o_{b1,2} = 1 - e^2\cos^2\omega_i \pm e\Theta_i\sin\omega_i \]
Recognizing that $1 - e^2\cos^2\omega_i = \Theta_i^2$, we extract the exact factorization:
\begin{equation}
1 + e\cos o_{b1,2} = \Theta_i(\Theta_i \pm e\sin\omega_i)
\end{equation}

Substituting this into the local potential projection $\kappa_o^2$:
\begin{equation}
\kappa_{ob1,2}^2 = \frac{2\beta^2}{1-e^2} \Theta_i(\Theta_i \pm e\sin\omega_i)
\end{equation}

Next, we evaluate the phase factor for the $S^1$ local kinetic projection $(1 + e^2 + 2e\cos o_b)$:
\[ 1 + e^2 + 2e\cos o_{b1,2} = 1 + e^2 - 2e^2\cos^2\omega_i \pm 2e\Theta_i\sin\omega_i \]
This expression algebraically collapses into a perfect geometric square:
\[ \Theta_i^2 + e^2\sin^2\omega_i \pm 2e\Theta_i\sin\omega_i = (\Theta_i \pm e\sin\omega_i)^2 \]

Substituting this into the local kinetic projection $\beta_o^2$:
\begin{equation}
\beta_{ob1,2}^2 = \frac{\beta^2}{1-e^2} (\Theta_i \pm e\sin\omega_i)^2
\end{equation}
\end{proof}
