\subsection{Connection to Quantum Mechanics}
According to de Broglie's hypothesis, the wavelength of a particle is given by:
\begin{equation}
    \lambda_n = \frac{h}{p_n},
\end{equation}
where \( h \) is Planck's constant and \( p_n \) is the momentum of the electron on the \( n \)-th orbital.

Substituting this into our geometric condition (1), we obtain:
\begin{equation}
    n \frac{h}{p_n} = 2\pi r_n.

\begin{equation}

This provides a relationship between the electron's momentum and the orbital radius. While this result aligns with the Schrödinger equation, where quantization manifests as standing waves, our approach does not require this construction and operates independently.

\section{Physical Model of the Atom}
In the hydrogen atom, the electron is bound to the nucleus by the Coulomb force. Here, the Coulomb attraction provides the centripetal force necessary for the electron's stable orbital leading to: 
\begin{equation}
    \frac{p_n^2}{m_e r_n} = \frac{e^2}{4\pi \varepsilon_0 r_n^2},
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \( m_e \) is the electron mass,
    \item \( e \) is the elementary charge,
    \item \( \varepsilon_0 \) is the vacuum permittivity.
\end{itemize}
Rearranging, we get:
\begin{equation}
    p_n^2 = \frac{m_e e^2}{4\pi \varepsilon_0 r_n}.
\end{equation}
This equation connects the momentum to the orbital radius through physical constants.

\subsection{Quantization of the Orbital Radius}
We now have two expressions for \( p_n \):
\begin{itemize}
    \item From geometry: \( p_n = \frac{n \hbar}{r_n} \),
    \item From the force balance: \( p_n^2 = \frac{m_e e^2}{4\pi \varepsilon_0 r_n} \).
\end{itemize}
Substituting the geometric expression into the force balance equation:
\begin{equation}
    \left( \frac{n \hbar}{r_n} \right)^2 = \frac{m_e e^2}{4\pi \varepsilon_0 r_n}.
\end{equation}
Multiplying both sides by \( r_n^2 \):
\begin{equation}
    \frac{n^2 \hbar^2}{r_n} = \frac{m_e e^2}{4\pi \varepsilon_0}.
\end{equation}
Solving for \( r_n \):
\begin{equation}
    r_n = \frac{4\pi \varepsilon_0 n^2 \hbar^2}{m_e e^2} = n^2 a_0,
\end{equation}
where \( a_0 = \frac{4\pi \varepsilon_0 \hbar^2}{m_e e^2} \) is the Bohr radius. This result matches the quantized radii in Bohr's model.

\subsection{Calculation of Energy Levels}
The total energy of the electron is the sum of its kinetic and potential energies:
\begin{equation}
    E_n = K_n + U_n = \frac{p_n^2}{2 m_e} - \frac{e^2}{4\pi \varepsilon_0 r_n}.
\end{equation}
Using the expression for \( p_n^2 \) from equation (6):
\begin{equation}
    E_n = \frac{1}{2} \frac{m_e e^2}{4\pi \varepsilon_0 r_n} - \frac{e^2}{4\pi \varepsilon_0 r_n} = -\frac{1}{2} \frac{e^2}{4\pi \varepsilon_0 r_n}.
\end{equation}
Substituting \( r_n = n^2 a_0 \):
\begin{equation}
    E_n = -\frac{1}{2} \frac{e^2}{4\pi \varepsilon_0 n^2 a_0}.
\end{equation}
Since \( a_0 = \frac{4\pi \varepsilon_0 \hbar^2}{m_e e^2} \), we have:
\begin{equation}
    E_n = -\frac{m_e e^4}{8 \varepsilon_0^2 n^2 \hbar^2}.
\end{equation}
This is the standard expression for the energy levels in the hydrogen atom.

\section{Photoelectric Effect: Geometric Derivation}
\subsection{Experimental Observations}
The photoelectric effect occurs when light incident on a metal surface ejects electrons. Key observations:
\begin{itemize}
    \item There is a \textbf{threshold frequency} \( f_{\text{thresh}} \) below which no electrons are emitted, regardless of intensity.
    \item The \textbf{kinetic energy} of emitted electrons increases with photon frequency, but is independent of intensity.
    \item The number of ejected electrons depends on intensity but their energy does not.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Standard Quantum Mechanical Explanation}
In quantum mechanics, light consists of photons, each carrying energy:
\begin{equation}
    E_{\text{photon}} = h f.
\end{equation}
To eject an electron, this energy must exceed the \textbf{work function} \( W \), which is the minimum energy needed to remove an electron from the metal. The remaining energy is converted into the kinetic energy of the electron:
\begin{equation}
    E_{\text{kin}} = h f - W.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Geometric Interpretation}
In our model, electrons in the metal form \textbf{standing waves} due to boundary conditions imposed by the atomic lattice. These standing waves satisfy the condition:
\begin{equation}
    n \lambda_e = 2L,
\end{equation}
where \( L \) represents an effective confinement region for the electron.

The work function \( W \) corresponds to the characteristic energy of the electron's stationary wave state:
\begin{equation}
    W = \frac{h c}{\lambda_e}.
\end{equation}
For a photon with wavelength \( \lambda_f \), the energy is:
\begin{equation}
    E_{\text{photon}} = \frac{h c}{\lambda_f}.
\end{equation}
If this photon disrupts the standing wave structure, the excess energy contributes to the electron's kinetic energy:
\begin{equation}
    E_{\text{kin}} = \frac{h c}{\lambda_f} - \frac{h c}{\lambda_e}.
\end{equation}
This is mathematically equivalent to Einstein’s equation for the photoelectric effect.
