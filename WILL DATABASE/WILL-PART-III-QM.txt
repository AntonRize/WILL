\[ \frac{R_q}{r_n} = \frac{2n^2\hbar^2}{m_e^2 c^2 r_n^2} \]

\[ r_n = \frac{2n^2\hbar^2}{m_e^2 c^2 R_q} \]

\[ r_n = \frac{2n^2\hbar^2}{m_e^2 c^2} \left( \frac{4\pi\varepsilon_0 m_e c^2}{2e^2} \right) \]
All dependencies on $c$ and one factor of $m_e$ cancel, yielding the final expression for the quantized radii.
\end{proof}

\section{Photoelectric Effect: Geometric Derivation}
\subsection{Experimental Observations}
The photoelectric effect occurs when light incident on a metal surface ejects electrons. Key observations:
\begin{itemize}
    \item There is a \textbf{threshold frequency} \( f_{\text{thresh}} \) below which no electrons are emitted, regardless of intensity.
    \item The \textbf{kinetic energy} of emitted electrons increases with photon frequency, but is independent of intensity.
    \item The number of ejected electrons depends on intensity but their energy does not.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Standard Quantum Mechanical Explanation}
In quantum mechanics, light consists of photons, each carrying energy:
\begin{equation}
    E_{\text{photon}} = h f.
\end{equation}
To eject an electron, this energy must exceed the \textbf{work function} \( W \), which is the minimum energy needed to remove an electron from the metal. The remaining energy is converted into the kinetic energy of the electron:
\begin{equation}
    E_{\text{kin}} = h f - W.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Geometric Interpretation}
In our model, electrons in the metal form \textbf{standing waves} due to boundary conditions imposed by the atomic lattice. These standing waves satisfy the condition:
\begin{equation}
    n \lambda_e = 2L,
\end{equation}
where \( L \) represents an effective confinement region for the electron.

The work function \( W \) corresponds to the characteristic energy of the electron's stationary wave state:
\begin{equation}
    W = \frac{h c}{\lambda_e}.
\end{equation}
For a photon with wavelength \( \lambda_f \), the energy is:
\begin{equation}
    E_{\text{photon}} = \frac{h c}{\lambda_f}.
\end{equation}
If this photon disrupts the standing wave structure, the excess energy contributes to the electron's kinetic energy:
\begin{equation}
    E_{\text{kin}} = \frac{h c}{\lambda_f} - \frac{h c}{\lambda_e}.
\end{equation}
This is mathematically equivalent to Einstein’s equation for the photoelectric effect.

\section{The Geometric Origin of the Fine Structure Constant}

We now arrive at the central result of this paper. With the atomic length scale $a_0$ derived from pure geometry, we can calculate the electron's relational projections and uncover the identity of a fundamental constant of nature.

\begin{theorem}[The Fine Structure Constant as the Ground-State Kinetic Projection]
The fine structure constant, $\alpha$, is identical to the kinetic projection, $\beta_1$, of the electron in the ground state of the hydrogen atom.
\[
\boxed{\alpha = \beta_1}
\]
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
We use our geometric system to find the value of $\beta_1$. From the geometric closure condition, $\beta_1^2 = \kappa_1^2/2$. From the scale principle, $\kappa_1^2 = R_q/r_1 = R_q/a_0$. Therefore:
\[ \beta_1^2 = \frac{1}{2} \frac{R_q}{a_0} \]
Substituting the expressions for $R_q$ and $a_0$:
\[ \beta_1^2 = \frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{2e^2}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 m_e c^2} \right) \left( \frac{m_e e^2}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 \hbar^2} \right) \]
The terms simplify to:
\[ \beta_1^2 = \frac{e^4}{(4\pi\varepsilon_0)^2 \hbar^2 c^2} = \left( \frac{e^2}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 \hbar c} \right)^2 \]
We recognize the term in parentheses as the standard definition of the fine structure constant, $\alpha$.
\[ \beta_1^2 = \alpha^2 \implies \beta_1 = \alpha \]
The theorem is proven.
\end{proof}
