\section{Introduction: The Tensor Captivity}

\subsection{The Standard Model of Inertia: The Tensor as a Descriptive "Black Box"}

In classical mechanics, inertia is the intrinsic resistance of a physical object to any change in its state of motion. For translational motion, this property is captured by a single scalar: mass. For rotational dynamics, however, the situation becomes substantially more complex. The rotational inertia of a body is described by the \textbf{moment of inertia tensor}, a \(3 \times 3\) matrix denoted by \(I_{ij}\).

This tensor functions as a linear operator that connects the angular velocity vector \(\vec{\omega}\) of a body to its angular momentum vector \(\vec{L}\):
\begin{equation}
    L_i = \sum_{j} I_{ij} \omega_j
\end{equation}
In this framework, the tensor is a powerful and essential tool for prediction. Given the nine components of \(I_{ij}\), one can accurately calculate the rich precessional and rotational dynamics of any rotating body. However, the tensor remains a purely \textit{descriptive} construct. It is a phenomenological "black box" populated with values derived from an object's mass distribution. It provides a precise "how" for rotational dynamics, but offers no insight into the fundamental "why." The tensor is a form of meticulous bookkeeping, not a physical explanation for the origin of inertia itself. This reliance on a postulated tensor to describe an object's behavior can be seen as a state of "tensor captivity."

\subsection{The Central Question: Is Inertia Intrinsic or Relational?}

The black-box nature of the inertia tensor forces a foundational question that has persisted for centuries: Is inertia an intrinsic property of an object, defined relative to an absolute and fixed space (Newton's view), or is it a \textit{relational} property, arising from the object's interaction with all other matter in the universe?

This latter view was most famously articulated by Ernst Mach. According to \textbf{Mach's Principle}, the inertia of any body is determined by the ensemble of all matter, often poetically summarized as the "distant stars." In this view, there is no absolute space for acceleration to be measured against; an object's inertia is a measure of its coupling to the rest of the cosmos. General Relativity (GR) was heavily influenced by this idea and incorporates Machian elements, most notably in the phenomenon of frame-dragging. Yet, it is widely debated whether GR fully implements Mach's principle, as it still permits solutions in an otherwise empty universe. The question of inertia's origin remains open.

\subsection{The Objective: Derivation from a Single Postulate}

The objective of this paper is to move beyond the descriptive tensor model and formally derive the phenomena of inertia from first principles. We will propose a new framework based on a single, irreducible postulate: \(\text{SPACETIME} \equiv \text{ENERGY EVOLUTION}\).

This postulate eliminates the need for a pre-existing spacetime manifold. Instead, it posits that all physical properties, including inertia, are emergent consequences of a universal, scale-invariant geometry of energy. We will demonstrate that this framework allows us to calculate, not postulate, the key effects traditionally attributed to inertiaâ€”from classical acceleration to relativistic frame-dragging. By doing so, we aim to liberate the concept of inertia from its tensor captivity and re-establish it as a predictable, relational phenomenon.
