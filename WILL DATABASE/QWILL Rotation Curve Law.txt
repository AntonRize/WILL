We document, with complete methodological transparency, the derivation of the compact galactic rotation law
\[

\section{Foundational Relations of WILL Geometry}

The WILL framework postulates the equivalence
\begin{equation}
\text{SPACETIME} \equiv \text{ENERGY EVOLUTION},
\end{equation}
and encodes this in the geometric invariant
\begin{equation}
\kappa^2(r) = \frac{R_s}{r_d} = \frac{\rho(r)}{\rho_{\max}(r)}.
\label{eq:kappa_def}
\end{equation}
Here:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\kappa$ is the \emph{temporal projection parameter},
    \item $R_s = \frac{2Gm_0}{c^2}$ is the Schwarzschild scale associated with $m_0$,
    \item $\rho_{\max}(r) = \frac{c^2}{8\pi G r^2}$ is the maximum admissible energy density at radius $r$.
\end{itemize}

The spatial projection parameter $\beta$ is related to $\kappa$ via the topological constraint:
\begin{equation}
\kappa^2 = 2\,\beta^2,
\label{eq:kappa_beta_relation}
\end{equation}
so that the \emph{total projection norm} becomes
\begin{equation}
Q^2 \equiv \beta^2 + \kappa^2 = \beta^2 + 2\beta^2 = 3\beta^2.
\label{eq:Q_def}
\end{equation}

\section{From Density to Rotation Velocity}

In Newtonian form, for a spherically symmetric system, the circular velocity $V_c(r)$ is related to the enclosed mass $M(r)$ as
\begin{equation}
V_c^2(r) = \frac{G M(r)}{r}.
\label{eq:Vc_newton}
\end{equation}

From \eqref{eq:kappa_def} and the definition of $R_s$, the enclosed mass in WILL geometry is
\begin{equation}
M(r) = \frac{\kappa^2 c^2 r}{2G}.
\label{eq:M_kappa}
\end{equation}

Substituting \eqref{eq:M_kappa} into \eqref{eq:Vc_newton} gives
\begin{equation}
V_c^2(r) = \frac{G}{r} \cdot \frac{\kappa^2 c^2 r}{2G}
= \frac{\kappa^2 c^2}{2}.
\label{eq:Vc_kappa}
\end{equation}

Thus, the Newtonian velocity term is directly related to $\kappa$ by:
\begin{equation}
V_c^2(r) = \beta^2 c^2
\quad\text{with}\quad \beta^2 = \frac{\kappa^2}{2}.
\label{eq:beta_def}
\end{equation}

\section{Baryonic Velocity in SPARC Formalism}

The SPARC database provides the \emph{baryonic velocity squared} as
\begin{equation}
V_{\mathrm{bary}}^2(r) =
V_{\mathrm{gas}}^2(r) +
\Upsilon_* \big[ V_{\mathrm{disk}}^2(r) + V_{\mathrm{bulge}}^2(r) \big],
\label{eq:Vbary_def}
\end{equation}
where $\Upsilon_*$ is the stellar mass-to-light ratio.

In the context of \eqref{eq:beta_def}, we identify:
\begin{equation}
V_{\mathrm{bary}}(r) \equiv \beta(r)\,c.
\label{eq:Vbary_beta}
\end{equation}

\section{Total Projection Velocity: QWILL}

The WILL framework includes both the spatial component ($\beta$) and the temporal projection component ($\kappa$).
The total projection magnitude is $Q$ from \eqref{eq:Q_def}:
\begin{equation}
Q^2 = 3\beta^2.
\end{equation}

The corresponding total velocity is then:
\begin{align}
V_{\mathrm{QWILL}}^2(r) &= Q^2\,c^2 \\
&= 3\beta^2 c^2 \\

so that
\begin{equation}
\boxed{
V_{\mathrm{QWILL}}(r) = \sqrt{3}\, V_{\mathrm{bary}}(r)

\label{eq:QWILL_final}
\end{equation}

Equation \eqref{eq:QWILL_final} shows that the complex original WILL velocity formula, which involved an explicit radial integral over the baryonic velocity profile, reduces in the SPARC data context to a fixed multiplicative factor $\sqrt{3}$ applied to the baryonic velocity curve.

This reduction is not an empirical fit but a direct consequence of:

    \item The projectional equivalence $\kappa^2 = 2\beta^2$,
    \item The identification $V_{\mathrm{bary}} = \beta\,c$ from the SPARC baryonic decomposition,
    \item The geometric closure relation $Q^2 = \beta^2 + \kappa^2$.

\section{Numerical Verification}

Applying \eqref{eq:QWILL_final} to 175 galaxies in the SPARC sample yields a median RMSE of $20.24\ \mathrm{km\,s^{-1}}$, comparable to the $18.83\ \mathrm{km\,s^{-1}}$ achieved by the full WILL integral model. This appendix provides a full derivation of the integral form used to compute the rotational velocity in the WILL framework:

  \item Observable baryonic term: \(\boxed{\beta^2 c^2 = V_{\text{bary}}^2(r)}\).

\subsection*{A.2 Energy Curvature Relation in WILL Geometry}

We begin with the key geometric definition of the curvature parameter \(\kappa^2(r)\), derived from the energy-mass integral:

\begin{equation}
\kappa^2(r) = \frac{2G}{c^2} \cdot \frac{1}{r} \int_0^r 4\pi r'^2 \rho(r') \, dr'.
\label{eq:kappa_def}
\end{equation}

This follows directly from the relation between \(\kappa^2\) and the Schwarzschild radius:
\[
\kappa^2 = \frac{R_s(r)}{r} = \frac{2G M(r)}{c^2 r}, \quad
\text{where} \quad
M(r) = \int_0^r 4\pi r'^2 \rho(r') \, dr'.
\]

\subsection*{A.3 Baryonic Energy and the \(\beta^2\) Term}

The observed baryonic mass components contribute a projected energy component \(\beta^2 c^2\), which is empirically constructed from gas, disk, and bulge velocity components via:

\begin{equation}
V_{\text{bary}}^2(r) = V_{\text{gas}}^2(r) + \Upsilon_* \left[ V_{\text{disk}}^2(r) + V_{\text{bulge}}^2(r) \right].
\label{eq:v_bary_def}
\end{equation}

Therefore, by definition:
\[
\boxed{\beta^2 c^2 = V_{\text{bary}}^2(r)}.
\]

\subsection*{A.4 Rewriting \(\kappa^2\) in Terms of \(V_{\text{bary}}^2\)}

Assuming the baryonic energy dominates the energy density \(\rho(r)\), we can express \(\rho(r)\) in terms of \(V_{\text{bary}}^2(r)\). Specifically, noting that \(V_{\text{bary}}^2(r)\) encodes kinetic energy of the luminous matter, we write:

\[
\rho(r) \propto V_{\text{bary}}^2(r),
\]
and so:

\[
\kappa^2(r) \propto \frac{1}{r} \int_0^r V_{\text{bary}}^2(r') \, dr'.
\]

This motivates the definition of an effective gravitational energy contribution term:
\[
V_{\text{grav}}^2(r) \equiv \lambda \cdot \frac{1}{r} \int_0^r 2 V_{\text{bary}}^2(r') \, dr',
\]
where \(\lambda\) is a dimensionless scaling factor absorbing constants of proportionality, and the factor of 2 maintains normalization with WILL geometry conventions.

\subsection*{A.5 Total Velocity and the WILL Integral Equation}

The total observable rotation velocity in the WILL framework is then given by the sum of kinetic (baryonic) and geometric (gravitational) contributions:

\[
V_{\text{WILL}}^2(r) = 2 V_{\text{bary}}^2(r) + V_{\text{grav}}^2(r)
= 2 V_{\text{bary}}^2(r) +
\lambda \cdot \frac{1}{r} \int_0^r 2 V_{\text{bary}}^2(r') \, dr'.
\]

This is the **integral velocity equation** used in all numerical fits.

\subsection*{A.6 Special Case: Surface Density Scaling \(\rho(r) \propto r^{-2}\)}

For the density profile:
\[
\rho(r) = \frac{\rho_0}{r^2},
\]
we find the enclosed mass:
\[
M(r) = \int_0^r 4\pi r'^2 \frac{\rho_0}{r'^2} \, dr' = 4\pi \rho_0 r,
\]
and thus:
\[
\kappa^2(r) = \frac{2G}{c^2} \cdot \frac{M(r)}{r} = \text{const}.
\]

This implies that \(\kappa^2\) is constant with radius, and that the geometric curvature contribution behaves identically to the \(\beta^2\) term, leading to:

\[
Q^2 = \kappa^2 + \beta^2 = 2\beta^2,
\]
and with the additional factor from the projection invariant:
\[
\boxed{V_{\text{QWILL}}^2 = Q^2 c^2 = 3 \beta^2 c^2 = 3 V_{\text{bary}}^2}.
\]

Hence, the rotation velocity becomes:

\[
\boxed{
V_{\text{QWILL}}(r) = \sqrt{3} \cdot V_{\text{bary}}(r)
}
\]
\[
\boxed{V_{\text{QWILL}}^2 = Q^2 c^2 = (\kappa^2 + \beta^2) c^2 = 3 \beta^2 c^2}

\subsection*{Closure Function and Morphological Dependence}

In the ideal weak--field regime the closure relation of the model yields
\begin{equation}
Q^2 \;=\; \beta^2 + \kappa^2 \,, \qquad \kappa^2 = 2\beta^2 \,,
\end{equation}
so that
\begin{equation}
\frac{g_{\rm obs}}{g_{\rm bar}} \;=\; \frac{Q^2}{\beta^2} \;=\; 1 + \frac{\kappa^2}{\beta^2} \;=\; 3 \,.
\end{equation}
This leads directly to the universal prediction
\begin{equation}
V_{\rm obs} \;=\; \sqrt{3}\, V_{\rm bary} \,,
\end{equation}
which holds as an asymptotic limit.

However, when applied to observed rotation curves, one finds systematic deviations in certain regimes (notably in intermediate--mass disks). This motivates defining an effective \emph{closure function}
\begin{equation}
C_{\rm eff} \;\equiv\; \frac{\kappa^2}{\beta^2} \;=\; \frac{g_{\rm obs}}{g_{\rm bar}} - 1 \,.
\end{equation}
By construction, $C_{\rm eff}=2$ in the pure geometric limit, but empirical data indicate that $C_{\rm eff}$ can vary with galaxy morphology.

A natural interpretation is that disk structure (bars, bulges, surface density) modifies the partition of the geometric budget, reducing $C_{\rm eff}$ below the ideal value of $2$ in certain classes of galaxies. Thus the generalized prediction is
\begin{equation}
V_{\rm obs} \;=\; \sqrt{\,1+C(\mathcal{M})\,}\;V_{\rm bary}\,,
\end{equation}
where $C(\mathcal{M})$ is a universal function of a morphological indicator $\mathcal{M}$ (such as stellar surface density or an effective proxy like $V_{\rm first}$). The asymptotic limit remains $C\to 2$, ensuring consistency with the underlying symmetry, while intermediate systems exhibit $C<2$ due to structural effects.

This formulation preserves the geometric principle of the model while making explicit the role of morphology in shaping galactic dynamics.
