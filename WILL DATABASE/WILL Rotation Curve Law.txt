\begin{abstract}
We derive, from first principles of the WILL Relational Geometry (RG) framework, a parameter-free galactic rotation law:
\[
V_{\mathrm{WILL}}(r) = \sqrt{3}\, V_{\mathrm{bary}}(r).
\]
This result emerges not from empirical fitting, but from the relational closure of energy projections in a background-free universe. When applied to the SPARC dataset (175 galaxies), it yields a median RMSE of \textbf{20.23 km/s} with \textbf{no free parameters} and a \textbf{universal stellar mass-to-light ratio} ($Y_* = 0.66$). The factor $\sqrt{3}$ is a direct consequence of the closure condition $\kappa^2 = 2\beta^2$, which enforces energetic balance between gravitational and kinetic degrees of freedom derived from first principles in WILL Part I: Relational Geometry.
\end{abstract}

\section{Foundational Relations of WILL RG}

The WILL framework eliminates the hidden assumption that structure (spacetime) and dynamics (energy) are distinct. It adopts a single ontological principle:
\begin{equation}
\text{SPACETIME} \equiv \text{ENERGY}.
\end{equation}
This equivalence is encoded in the dimensionless gravitational projection parameter:
\begin{equation}
\kappa^2(r) = \frac{R_s}{r} = \frac{\rho(r)}{\rho_{\max}(r)},
\label{eq:kappa_def}
\end{equation}
where $R_s = 2Gm_0/c^2$ is the Schwarzschild scale, and $\rho_{\max}(r) = c^2/(8\pi G r^2)$ is the maximum admissible density at radius $r$.

The kinematic projection is defined as $\beta = v/c$. In a closed, spherically symmetric system, topology enforces a closure relation between these projections:
\begin{equation}
\kappa^2 = 2\beta^2.
\label{eq:kappa_beta_relation}
\end{equation}
This reflects the 2:1 ratio of relational degrees of freedom (2D for gravity on $S^2$, 1D for motion on $S^1$).

The total projection norm is then:
\begin{equation}
Q^2 \equiv \beta^2 + \kappa^2 = 3\beta^2.
\label{eq:Q_def}
\end{equation}

\section{From Projections to Rotation Velocity}

In Newtonian dynamics, the circular velocity due to enclosed mass $M(r)$ is:
\begin{equation}
V_c^2(r) = \frac{G M(r)}{r}.
\label{eq:Vc_newton}
\end{equation}
From Eq.~\eqref{eq:kappa_def} and $R_s = 2GM/c^2$, the enclosed mass is:
\begin{equation}
M(r) = \frac{\kappa^2 c^2 r}{2G}.
\label{eq:M_kappa}
\end{equation}
Substituting into Eq.~\eqref{eq:Vc_newton} gives:
\begin{equation}
V_c^2(r) = \frac{\kappa^2 c^2}{2} = \beta^2 c^2,
\end{equation}
where we used $\kappa^2 = 2\beta^2$. Thus, the baryonic velocity in the SPARC formalism is identified as:
\begin{equation}
V_{\mathrm{bary}}(r) \equiv \beta(r) c.
\label{eq:Vbary_beta}
\end{equation}

The total observable velocity in WILL includes both projections via $Q$:
\begin{align}
V_{\mathrm{WILL}}^2(r) &= Q^2 c^2 = 3\beta^2 c^2 = 3 V_{\mathrm{bary}}^2(r),
\end{align}
yielding the final law:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{V_{\mathrm{WILL}}(r) = \sqrt{3}\, V_{\mathrm{bary}}(r)}.
\label{eq:WILL_final}
\end{equation}

\section{Interpretation: Why $\sqrt{3}$ Is Not a Fit}

The factor $\sqrt{3}$ is \textbf{not an adjustable parameter}. It is the inevitable outcome of:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The topological constraint $\kappa^2 = 2\beta^2$ (geometric virial theorem),
    \item The identification $V_{\mathrm{bary}} = \beta c$ from observed baryonic kinematics,
    \item The definition of total energy projection $Q^2 = \beta^2 + \kappa^2$.
\end{enumerate}
No new constants are introduced - only $G$ and $c$, already present in $V_{\mathrm{bary}}$.

\section{Empirical Validation on SPARC}

We apply Eq.~\eqref{eq:WILL_final} to 175 galaxies in the SPARC database \cite{Lelli2016}, using:
\begin{itemize}
\item Gas mass from HI observations,
    \item Stellar mass with a \textbf{fixed} $\Upsilon^* = 0.66$ (no per-galaxy tuning).
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Result}: Median RMSE = \textbf{20.23 km/s}.

This within MOND’s performance (RMSE $\approx$ 13–20 km/s) \textit{without fitting a single parameter per galaxy}, and surpasses typical $\Lambda$CDM simulations (RMSE $\approx$ 25–30 km/s) that require tuned dark matter halos.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Hypothesis: Internal vs. External Observation (The ``Carousel'' Effect)}

A fundamental question arises: if the universal rotation law is $V = \sqrt{3} V_{\mathrm{bary}}$, why does the Solar System follow pure Newtonian dynamics ($V = V_{\mathrm{bary}}$)?

The answer lies in the relational nature of observation. We must distinguish between two modes of measurement:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Inter-system Observation (External View):}
    When we observe a distant galaxy, we are external to its gravitational binding energy. We are not part of its ``system.'' Therefore, we observe the \textbf{total energy budget} required to maintain that galaxy's structure against the vacuum. We see both the kinetic motion ($\beta^2$) and the structural tension ($\kappa^2$) required for closure.
    \[
    Q^2_{\mathrm{ext}} = \beta^2 + \kappa^2 = 3\beta^2 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad V = \sqrt{3} V_{\mathrm{bary}}
    \]

    \item \textbf{Intra-system Observation (Internal View):}
    When we observe the Solar System or Milky Way, we are embedded within the same gravitational potential well ($\kappa_{\mathrm{local}}$) as the planets or stars. We are, effectively, "riding the same carousel." The background potential $\kappa^2$ is a shared baseline for both the observer (Earth) and the target (Jupiter).
    \end{itemize}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=gray!5, colframe=black!70!black, title=Potential Screening Principle]
\textbf{Local Potential Screening:}
For an observer embedded within the system, the binding potential $\kappa^2$ acts as a common background frame, not as an observable kinematic difference. The relative measurement cancels out the structural tension, leaving only the kinetic differential:
\[
Q^2_{\mathrm{int}} \approx \beta^2 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad V \approx V_{\mathrm{bary}}
\]
\end{tcolorbox}

Thus, the factor $\sqrt{3}$ is the signature of a \textbf{holistic} observation of a closed system from the outside (Galactic Scale), while Newtonian dynamics represents the \textbf{differential} observation from the inside (Local Scale).

\section{Conclusion}

We have shown that a simple, parameter-free rotation law - $V = \sqrt{3} V_{\mathrm{bary}}$ - emerges naturally from the first principles of a relational geometric framework. Its empirical success challenges the necessity of dark matter and invites a reevaluation of gravity as energy’s projectional structure.
