\section{Energy-Symmetry Law}

\subsection{Causal Continuity and Energy Symmetry}

\begin{theorem}[Energy Symmetry]
The specific energy differences (per unit of rest energy) perceived by two observers for a transition between their states balance according to the Energy-Symmetry Law:
\begin{equation}
\Delta E_{A \to B} + \Delta E_{B \to A} = 0.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Consider two observers:
\begin{itemize}
\item Observer $A$ at rest on the surface at radius $r_A$ (state defined by $\kappa_A, \beta_A=0$).
\item Observer $B$ orbiting at radius $r_B > r_A$ with orbital velocity $v_B$ (state defined by $\kappa_B, \beta_B$).
\end{itemize}
Each observer perceives energy transfers as the sum of the change in potential and kinetic energy budgets.

From $A$'s perspective (transition from surface to orbit):
\begin{enumerate}
\item An object gains potential energy by moving away from the gravitational center.
\item It gains kinetic energy by accelerating to orbital velocity.
\end{enumerate}
The total specific energy required for this transition is the sum of these two contributions:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\Delta E_{A \to B} = \underbrace{\frac{1}{2}(\kappa_A^2 - \kappa_B^2)}_{\text{Change in Potential}} + \underbrace{\frac{1}{2}(\beta_B^2 - \beta_A^2)}_{\text{Change in Kinetic}}}
\end{equation}
Since observer A is at rest, $\beta_A = 0$, and the expression simplifies to:
\begin{equation}
\Delta E_{A \to B} = \frac{1}{2}((\kappa_A^2 - \kappa_B^2) + \beta_B^2)
\end{equation}

From $B$'s perspective (transition from orbit to surface):
\begin{enumerate}
\item The object loses potential energy descending into a stronger gravitational field.
\item It loses kinetic energy by reducing its velocity to rest.
\end{enumerate}
This results in a specific energy difference:
\begin{equation}
\Delta E_{B \to A} = \frac{1}{2}((\kappa_B^2 - \kappa_A^2) + \frac{1}{2}((\beta_A^2 - \beta_B^2)) = \frac{1}{2}((\kappa_B^2 - \kappa_A^2) - \beta_B^2)
\end{equation}

Summing these transfers gives:
\begin{equation}
\Delta E_{A \to B} + \Delta E_{B \to A} = 0
\end{equation}
Thus, no net energy is created or destroyed in a closed cycle of transitions, confirming the Energy-Symmetry Law as a direct consequence of the closed geometry.
\end{proof}

\subsection{The Specific Energy Transfer ($\Delta E$):} This is the projectional energy difference between states, equivalent to the Total Relational Shift $Q$, corresponding to the classical total energy of a transition (per unit rest energy). It is defined as the \textbf{sum of the changes} in the potential and kinetic energy budgets:

    \end{equation}
It is this quantity, $\Delta E$, that is conserved and must balance to zero in any closed system.
    
When the closure condition for stable, periodic orbits ($\kappa^2 - 2\beta^2 = 0$) is applied, the general Energy-Symmetry Law simplifies into remarkably elegant and direct forms. These simplified equations provide the precise energy balance for transitions involving energetically closed systems, such as planets or satellites in stable orbits.

\frac{E_{A \to B}}{E_{0B}} = \frac{1}{2}(\kappa_A^2 - \beta_B^2)
\end{equation}
This result is derived by applying the closure condition $\kappa_B^2 = 2\beta_B^2$ to the general energy transfer formula, elegantly linking the initial potential projection to the final kinetic projection.

In this case, applying the closure condition to both the initial and final orbits causes the potential projection terms ($\kappa^2$) to cancel out completely. If so, the algebraic difference of the two coefficients must vanish identically at first order, and the residual at finite values of the small parameters must reproduce the analytically derived second-order correction.

\subsection{Methodology}

The verification is split into two independent parts, both executed in a single public notebook (Section~\ref{sec:repro}).

\paragraph{Part I --- symbolic identity.} Introduce the dimensionless geometric ratio $\rho \equiv R_{E}/r_{\mathrm{GPS}}$, which is treated as a finite (order-unity) parameter and is \emph{not} expanded. The exact WILL~RG shift coefficient is then
\begin{equation}
\delta_{\mathrm{RG}}(\kappa_{E}^{2}, \beta_{E}^{2}, \rho) \;=\; 1 - \frac{\sqrt{(1-\kappa_{E}^{2})(1-\beta_{E}^{2})}}{\sqrt{(1-\kappa_{E}^{2}\rho)(1-\kappa_{E}^{2}\rho/2)}} .
\label{eq:delta-RG}
\end{equation}
A single bookkeeping parameter $\varepsilon$ is introduced via $\kappa_{E}^{2} \to \varepsilon\,\kappa_{E}^{2}$, $\beta_{E}^{2} \to \varepsilon\,\beta_{E}^{2}$, and the Taylor series of \eqref{eq:delta-RG} is computed to second order in $\varepsilon$ using \texttt{sympy.series}. The first-order coefficient $\delta_{\mathrm{RG}}^{(1)}$ is then subtracted from the dimensionless form of the GR coefficient $\delta_{\mathrm{GR}}$ obtained from \eqref{eq:dt-GR} under the substitutions $\Phi/c^{2} = -\kappa^{2}/2$ and $v^{2}/c^{2} = \beta^{2}$. The result is simplified algebraically.

\paragraph{Part II --- numerical comparison.} Both \eqref{eq:dt-GR} and \eqref{eq:dt-RG} are evaluated with 40-digit precision arithmetic (\texttt{mpmath}, \texttt{mp.dps = 40}) using a single set of input parameters: $c = 299{,}792{,}458\,\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$, $GM_{\oplus} = 3.986004418 \times 10^{14}\,\mathrm{m^{3}\,s^{-2}}$, $R_{E} = 6{,}378{,}137\,\mathrm{m}$, $r_{\mathrm{GPS}} = 26{,}561{,}750\,\mathrm{m}$, $D = 86{,}400\,\mathrm{s}$. The closure residual $\beta_{\mathrm{GPS}}^{2} - \kappa_{\mathrm{GPS}}^{2}/2$ is monitored as a numerical consistency check. The numerical discrepancy $\Delta t_{\mathrm{RG}} - \Delta t_{\mathrm{GR}}$ is then compared with the analytically predicted second-order correction $\delta_{\mathrm{RG}}^{(2)}\,D\,M$ obtained from Part~I.

\subsection{Results}

\paragraph{Symbolic.} The first-order Taylor coefficient of the WILL~RG shift, expressed in $\kappa_{E}^{2}$, $\beta_{E}^{2}$, and the geometric ratio $\rho$, is
\begin{equation}
\delta_{\mathrm{RG}}^{(1)} \;=\; \frac{\kappa_{E}^{2}}{2} \;+\; \frac{\beta_{E}^{2}}{2} \;-\; \frac{3\,\kappa_{E}^{2}\,\rho}{4} .
\label{eq:delta-RG-1}
\end{equation}
The GR coefficient, expressed in the same variables, is
\begin{equation}
\delta_{\mathrm{GR}} \;=\; \frac{\kappa_{E}^{2}}{2} \;+\; \frac{\beta_{E}^{2}}{2} \;-\; \frac{3\,\kappa_{E}^{2}\,\rho}{4} .
\label{eq:delta-GR}
\end{equation}
The algebraic difference $\delta_{\mathrm{RG}}^{(1)} - \delta_{\mathrm{GR}}$ vanishes identically.

The second-order content of \eqref{eq:delta-RG}, which is absent from the additive GR formula \eqref{eq:dt-GR}, is
\begin{equation}
\delta_{\mathrm{RG}}^{(2)} \;=\; \frac{\kappa_{E}^{4}}{8} \;+\; \frac{\beta_{E}^{4}}{8} \;-\; \frac{\beta_{E}^{2}\,\kappa_{E}^{2}}{4} \;+\; \frac{3\,\kappa_{E}^{4}\,\rho}{8} \;-\; \frac{19\,\kappa_{E}^{4}\,\rho^{2}}{32} \;+\; \frac{3\,\beta_{E}^{2}\,\kappa_{E}^{2}\,\rho}{8} .
\label{eq:delta-RG-2}
\end{equation}
Three structural features of \eqref{eq:delta-RG-2} have no analogue in any rearrangement of the additive GR sum~\eqref{eq:dt-GR}: (i)~the pure quadratic contributions $\kappa_{E}^{4}/8$ and $\beta_{E}^{4}/8$ arising from higher-order expansion of $\sqrt{1-x}$; (ii)~the cross-term $-\beta_{E}^{2}\kappa_{E}^{2}/4$, which originates from the multiplicative coupling of the gravitational and kinematic projection factors within $\tau_{E}/\tau_{\mathrm{GPS}}$; and (iii)~the $\rho^{2}$ contribution, which encodes nonlinear dependence on the relative depth of the gravitational well.

\paragraph{Numerical.} With the parameter set listed above:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{|l|r|}
\hline
quantity & value [$\mu$s\,day$^{-1}$] \\
\hline
$\Delta t_{\mathrm{RG}}$ \quad (exact)              & $38.5421472752$ \\\hline
$\Delta t_{\mathrm{GR}}$ \quad (first order)         & $38.5421472451$ \\\hline
$\Delta t_{\mathrm{RG}} - \Delta t_{\mathrm{GR}}$    & $3.017 \times 10^{-8}$ \\\hline
$\delta_{\mathrm{RG}}^{(2)} \cdot D\,M$ \;(predicted) & $3.017 \times 10^{-8}$ \\\hline
residual                                              & $\sim 10^{-17}$ \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The numerical discrepancy $\Delta t_{\mathrm{RG}} - \Delta t_{\mathrm{GR}}$ matches the analytically derived second-order correction $\delta_{\mathrm{RG}}^{(2)}\,D\,M$ to within the limit set by the 40-digit floating-point arithmetic. The closure residual $\beta_{\mathrm{GPS}}^{2} - \kappa_{\mathrm{GPS}}^{2}/2$ vanishes to $\sim 10^{-50}$. The relative magnitude of the term omitted by the GR additive formula is $\sim 7.8 \times 10^{-10}$ of the total shift.

\subsection{Interpretation}

Equations~\eqref{eq:delta-RG-1}--\eqref{eq:delta-GR} establish that the additive post-Newtonian formula~\eqref{eq:dt-GR} coincides exactly with the linear Taylor coefficient of the WILL~RG exact algebraic ratio~\eqref{eq:dt-RG}. This is an algebraic identity in $(\kappa_{E}^{2}, \beta_{E}^{2}, \rho)$, not a numerical near-match: the additive sum-of-corrections structure of \eqref{eq:dt-GR} is recovered as the unique leading-order content of the multiplicative ratio $\tau_{E}/\tau_{\mathrm{GPS}}$.

For the Earth--GPS configuration, the second-order term \eqref{eq:delta-RG-2} contributes at the level of $\sim 3 \times 10^{-8}\,\mu\mathrm{s\,day}^{-1}$, i.e.\ $\sim 8 \times 10^{-10}$ of the total accumulated shift. This is below the present precision of operational GPS clock comparisons and does not, by itself, constitute a distinguishing observation between the two formulations in this regime. The structural difference between an additive and a multiplicative composition of projection factors --- in particular the presence of the $\beta^{2}\kappa^{2}$ cross-term in \eqref{eq:delta-RG-2} --- becomes quantitatively relevant only when $\kappa^{2}$ or $\beta^{2}$ approach order unity.

The scope of the present statement is restricted to the relation between the WILL~RG closed-form expression~\eqref{eq:dt-RG} and the \emph{additive post-Newtonian} form of the GR time-dilation formula \eqref{eq:dt-GR}. A comparison against fully non-linear geodesic integration in the exact Schwarzschild geometry combined with special-relativistic kinematics for the rotating Earth surface is a separate computation and is not addressed in this section.
