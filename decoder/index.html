The Holographic Decoder

A telescope watches a star and measures how the frequency of its light shifts over time. That is all we collect. From that single stream of numbers this page reconstructs the complete three-dimensional orbit — its shape, its tilt, its size in kilometres, and the mass of the invisible partner pulling on the star — using only the speed of light and two dimensionless ratios, β and κ. Newton's constant G and the mass M never enter the chain. They reappear only at the very end, as a translation into familiar units.

Act I

Here is all you get

We record how the frequency of a star's light changes with time. That is all the information we will use to decode its world.

The simulator generates a realistic radial-velocity dataset for a star bound to a compact invisible companion. The underlying orbit is treated to first post-Newtonian order, so the light carries the classical Keplerian motion plus three general-relativistic corrections: the gravitational redshift from the companion's potential well, the transverse Doppler shift from the star's orbital speed, and the relativistic precession of the orbit's pericentre. The simulator keeps drawing new system parameters until the relativistic part of the signal rises clearly above the measurement noise. The exported file holds only what any real spectroscopic survey would publish: the time of each observation, the measured line-of-sight velocity in km/s, and its uncertainty in km/s.

Act III

    
The relational decoding

  


  

    The coherent wave left over from Act II is not noise and not a
    mystery — it is the signature of two dimensionless ratios,
    β (how fast the star
    moves, as a fraction of the speed of light) and
    κ (how deep it sits
    in the companion's gravity well). The decoder now searches for the
    single pair (β, κ) that explains both the Keplerian rhythm and
    the residual wave at once, then walks a Markov chain around that point
    to measure how tightly the data pin it down.
  


  

    Stage 2 runs a 7-parameter differential-evolution search over
    (β, i, vz₀, e, ω, P, T_peri) against the full R.O.M. forward model
    Z_sys(o)·(1+β_los)·(1+vz₀/c), with phase projections
    β_o² = β²(1+e²+2e cos o)/(1−e²) and κ_o² = 2β²(1+e cos o)/(1−e²),
    and exact phase-accumulated precession ω(o) = ω₀ + (τ_Y²/(1−e²))·o.
    Stage 3 initialises 32 emcee walkers in a 1e−6 ball around the
    Stage-2 peak and samples the posterior; running medians update as
    each chunk completes. The Decryption Invariant (Eq. 3.123) is
    evaluated at every finalised point and its residual |LHS−2| is

Act IV

    
What we have just done

  


  

    

      We began with a telescope pointed at a single star and a stream of
      numbers — how the frequency of its light drifted over time. One
      dimension. A scalar function of one variable. By every classical
      accounting this is the most impoverished signal in astrophysics:
      the famous M sin i degeneracy says that mass and
      inclination cannot be separated from radial velocities alone.
    

    

      And yet the table above lists the full three-dimensional orbit —
      its eccentricity, its tilt, its period, the orientation of its
      long axis, the Schwarzschild radius of the companion, the
      semi-major axis in astronomical units, the pericentre distance,
      the rate at which the orbit's axis turns century after century.
      None of these numbers were fit independently. They all fell out
      of a single closed-form identity
      (Eq. 3.123) tying the observed redshift
      extrema to two dimensionless ratios (β, κ) that obey one law:
      κ² = 2β².
    

    

      The only physical constant we used was the speed of light.
      Newton's G never appeared. The companion's mass M never appeared.
      The legacy mass in the last row of the table is a translation, not
      a measurement — a sentence rewritten into an older language after
      the book has already been read.
    


    


    

      The usual way to describe this would be: we compressed a 3D
      reality into a 1D signal and then decompressed it. That
      description is wrong, and the wrongness is the point.
    

    

      A 1D stream cannot contain a 3D reality — not in any
      bit-counting sense. What the decoding shows is that the
      three-dimensional picture was never the fundamental object. The
      orbit, the axis, the inclination, the mass — these are not things
      the universe stores; they are things we reconstruct from
      relational invariants that live on a lower-dimensional carrier.
      Dimensionality is a property of representation, not of
      information. The geometry we thought was out there, waiting to be
      measured, was already encoded in the relational shift
      Q² = κ² + β² = 3β² — a single
      dimensionless number that breathes with orbital phase along the
      line κ² = 2β².
    

    

      Consider what just happened, in the most deflationary terms
      possible. A number line drifted over days. From that number line,
      a browser with no access to Newton's constant reconstructed the
      size of a black hole, the shape of an orbit, the tilt of a plane
      it cannot see, and the rate at which that plane will turn over
      the next hundred years. It did this by insisting on one equation
      — κ² = 2β² — and refusing to
      use mass as a primitive.
    

    

      Mass, in other words, was not needed to describe gravity. It was
      only needed to translate the answer back into a vocabulary
      inherited from Newton. The decoder treats M the way a modern
      physicist treats phlogiston: a historical unit of account, not a
      constituent of nature.

        Not as metaphor. Not as slogan. As the only reading of the

        Spacetime is what relational energy looks like when you draw

      This page is a single experiment, not a proof. But any theory of
      gravity that still needs M as a primitive now has to explain why
      a dimensionless closure on a 1D data stream was enough.
