The Holographic Decoder

A telescope watches a star and measures how the frequency of its light shifts over time. That is all we collect. From that single stream of numbers this page reconstructs the complete three-dimensional orbit — its shape, its tilt, its size in kilometres, and the mass of the invisible partner pulling on the star — using only the speed of light and two dimensionless ratios, β and κ. Newton's constant G and the mass M never enter the chain. They reappear only at the very end, as a translation into familiar units.

Act I

Here is all you get

We record how the frequency of a star's light changes with time. That is all the information we will use to decode its world.

The simulator generates a realistic radial-velocity dataset for a star bound to a compact invisible companion. The underlying orbit is treated to first post-Newtonian order, so the light carries the classical Keplerian motion plus three general-relativistic corrections: the gravitational redshift from the companion's potential well, the transverse Doppler shift from the star's orbital speed, and the relativistic precession of the orbit's pericentre. The simulator keeps drawing new system parameters until the relativistic part of the signal rises clearly above the measurement noise. The exported file holds only what any real spectroscopic survey would publish: the time of each observation, the measured line-of-sight velocity in km/s, and its uncertainty in km/s.

Act II

    
The classical skeleton

  


  

    A classical Kepler orbit — the kind Newton would have written down —
    captures the bulk of what the light is doing. It explains the rising
    and falling velocity. It finds a period, an eccentricity, an angle.
    But look at what it cannot explain. What remains after subtracting
    the Kepler curve is not random noise.
    It is a coherent wave, locked to the orbit's own rhythm. Something
    systematic is hiding in the light, and classical mechanics alone
    has no name for it.
  


  

    A six-parameter Keplerian radial-velocity model
    (semi-amplitude, systemic velocity, eccentricity, pericentre angle,
    period, time of pericentre) is fit to the data by differential
    evolution. The residuals — observed velocity minus best classical
    fit — are shown below as a function of time. Their amplitude and
    the fact that they are phase-locked to the orbit, not white, is
    the signature of the relativistic baseline and the secular
    pericentre precession that a purely Newtonian model is structurally
    incapable of producing.

Act III

    
The relational decoding

  


  

    The small wave Act II could not explain is not noise. It carries
    two physical quantities: how fast the star actually moves along
    its orbit, and how deep it sits inside the companion's gravity
    well at every instant. The decoder now searches for the single
    pair of numbers that explains both the classical wobble and the
    leftover wave at once — and then walks a Markov chain around that
    point to measure how tightly the data pin the answer down.
  


  

    Stage 2 runs a seven-parameter global search against the full
    relational forward model for the observed light-frequency ratio,
    including the systemic gravitational redshift, the transverse
    Doppler effect, the line-of-sight Doppler shift, and the
    relativistic precession of the orbit's major axis accumulated
    exactly through each revolution. Stage 3 then runs a Markov-chain
    sampler in a tight local prior around the Stage 2 peak to measure
    the posterior width on every parameter. The closed-form identity

Act IV

    
What just happened

  


  

    

      We started with one number drifting over time — the frequency of
      a star's light. We finished with its orbit, eccentricity, period,
      Schwarzschild radius, pericentre, and century of precession.
    

    

      The only constant used was c. Newton's G and the companion's mass
      M never entered the fit. The legacy mass in the last row is a
      translation, computed after the fact from
      M = Rsc²/(2G).
    

    

      The reconstruction rests on one relation —
      κ² = 2β² — and one closed-form
